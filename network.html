Chapter 4 Terms

Definition


Network Classifications
  • LAN
    		•  A local area network (LAN) consists of a series of computers linked together to form a network in a circumscribed location. The computers in a LAN connect to each other via TCP/IP ethernet or Wi-Fi.
  • MAN
    		•  A metropolitan area network (MAN) is a computer network that connects computers within a metropolitan area, which could be a single large city, multiple cities and towns, or any given large area with multiple buildings
  • WAN
    		•  A communications network that connects computing devices over geographically dispersed locations

    Network Models
  • Bus Network
    		•  A local area network (LAN) topology in which each node -- a workstation or other device -- is connected to a main cable or link called a bus.
  • Star Network
    		•  A local area network (LAN) topology in which all nodes -- personal computers (PCs), workstations or other devices -- are directly connected to a common central computer that is often referred to as a hub. Therefore, a star network is often referred to as a hub-and-spoke network topology.


    Protocols
  • CSMA/CD Ethernet
    		•  (Carrier Sense Multiple Access With Collision Detection) CSMA/CD (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision detection) is a Media Access Control (MAC) protocol used in Local Area Networking: It uses early Ethernet technology to overcome collision when it occurs.
  • CSMA/CA Wifi
    		•  Carrier sense multiple access/collision avoidance (CSMA/CA) is a protocol for carrier transmission in 802.11 networks. It was developed to minimize the potential of a collision occurring when two or more stations send their signals over a data link layer.

    Combining Networks
  • Repeater
    		•  A network device that retransmits a received signal with more power and to an extended geographical or topological network boundary than what would be capable with the original signal.
  • Bridge
    		•  A class of network device that's designed to connect networks at OSI Level 2, which is the data link layer of a local-area network (LAN).
  • Switch
    		•  A high-speed device that receives incoming data packets and redirects them to their destination on a local area network (LAN).
  • Router
    		•  A computer that is a gateway between two networks at OSI layer 3 and that relays and directs data packets through that inter-network. The most common form of router operates on IP packets.
  • Gateway
    		•  An intermediate system (interface, relay) that attaches to two (or more) computer networks that have similar functions but dissimilar implementations and that enables either one-way or two-way communication between the networks.


    Internet Addressing
  • Tier 1
    		•  An Internet Protocol (IP) network that can reach every other network on the Internet solely via settlement-free interconnection (also known as settlement-free peering)
  • Tier 2
    		•  A service provider that utilizes a combination of paid transit via Tier 1 ISPs and peering with other Tier 2 ISPs to deliver Internet traffic to end customers through Tier 3 ISPs. Tier 2 ISPs are typically regional or national providers.
  • Tier 3
    		•  Primarily engaged in delivering Internet access to end customers. Tier 3 ISPs focus on local business and consumer market conditions. They provide the "on-ramp" or local access to the Internet for end customers, through cable, DSL, fiber or wireless access networks.

    IP Addressing
  • ICANN
    		•  (Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers) is the private, non-government, nonprofit corporation with responsibility for Internet Protocol (IP) address space allocation, protocol parameter assignment, domain name system (DNS) management and root server system management functions.
  • DNS
    		•  A distributed database that stores information about entities on the Internet that are referenced by their domain name. Its most common use is to simply translate the domain name into its corresponding IP address which the network devices use to deliver the information over the Internet
  • TLD: Top Level Domain
    		•  The most generic domain in the Internet's hierarchical DNS (domain name system). A TLD is the final component of a domain name, for example, "org" in developer.mozilla.org
  • Country-Code TLDs
    		•  Indicates a country or geographic location of the website.


    Internet Protocols
  • FTP
    		•  File Transfer Protocol." FTP is a protocol designed for transferring files over the Internet
  • HTTP
    		•  HTTP, in full HyperText Transfer Protocol, standard application-level protocol used for exchanging files on the World Wide Web. HTTP runs on top of the TCP/IP protocol and (later) on the QUIC protocol.
  • SMTP
    		•  A messaging protocol governing electronic-mail transmission in Transmission Control Protocol/Internet Protocol (TCP/IP) networks. It is used to transfer e-mail between computers. It is a server-to-server protocol.

    World Wide Web
  • URL
    		•  Uniform Resource Locator. A URL is nothing more than the address of a given unique resource on the Web
  • Client-Side Activities
    		•  Everything in a web application that is displayed or takes place on the client (end user device).
  • Server-Side Activities
    		•  Activities that take place on an organization's web server such as processing forms, streaming video, and accessing product databases for sending pages to users.


    Internet Four-Layer Software
  • Application Layer
    		•  Sits at Layer 7, the top of the Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) communications model. It ensures an application can effectively communicate with other applications on different computer systems and networks.
  • Transport Layer
    		•  Layer of the TCP/IP protocol stack that is responsible for reliable connection-oriented or connectionless end-to-end communications.
  • Network Layer
    		•  Handles the routing and sending of data between different networks
  • Link Layer
    		•  Handles communications between devices on the same network.


    Internet is:
  • Packet-Switching Network
    		•  Refers to the ability of networking equipment to process packets independently from each other.
  • Fault Tolerant
    		•  Having the built-in capability to provide continued, correct execution of its assigned function in the presence of a hardware and/or software fault.
  • Redundant
    		•  A system design in which a component is duplicated so if it fails there will be a backup.


    CyberSecurity
  • Malware
    		•  Any program or file that is intentionally harmful to a computer, network or server.
  • Virus
    		•  A type of malware that can execute itself and spread by infecting other programs or files.
  • Worm
    		•  Can self-replicate without a host program and typically spreads without any interaction from the malware authors.
  • Trojan Horse
    		•  Designed to appear as a legitimate software program to gain access to a system. Once activated following installation, Trojans can execute their malicious functions.
  • Spyware
    		•  Collects information and data on the device and user, as well as observes the user's activity without their knowledge.
  • Phishing
    		•  A technique for attempting to acquire sensitive data, such as bank account numbers, through a fraudulent solicitation in email or on a web site, in which the perpetrator masquerades as a legitimate business or reputable person.
  • DDos Attack
    		•  Distributed denial of service (DDoS) attacks are a subclass of denial of service (DoS) attacks. A DDoS attack involves multiple connected online devices, collectively known as a botnet, which are used to overwhelm a target website with fake traffic.

    Protection
  • Firewall
    		•  A device that has a network protection application installed to safeguard the network from intentional or unintentional intrusion
  • Spam Filter
    		•  A program used to detect unsolicited, unwanted and virus-infected emails and prevent those messages from getting to a user's inbox
  • Proxy Server
    		•  A gateway between you and the internet. It's an intermediary server separating end users from the websites they browse. Proxy servers provide varying levels of functionality, security, and privacy depending on your use case, needs, or company policy.
  • Antivirus Software
    		•  A program that monitors a computer or network to identify all major types of malware and prevent or contain malware incidents.
  • Cryptography
    		•  The study of secure communications techniques that allow only the sender and intended recipient of a message to view its contents. The term is derived from the Greek word kryptos, which means hidden.
  • Public Key Encription
    		•  An encryption technique developed to overcome the limitations of secret-key cryptography
